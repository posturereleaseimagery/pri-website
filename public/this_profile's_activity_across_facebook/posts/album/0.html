This chapter is now complete but I will no doubt add to it over time, depending on what you readers need in the way of explanation. It is a very important segment to me because upon discovery of the parallel images and effects of some of my basic imagery to that of basic Christian images, I was very surprised. I had never thought of Christianity as being related to the mind/body somatic disciplines so popular now, from the Alexander Technique to Feldenkrais to Eastern religious or inspired disciplines such as Yoga, Tai Chi, Zen Buddhist meditation, and many more. I hope that Christians and non-Christians alike who are reading this will give the ideas and experiences enough time to work on you that you will have a well-informed view of what I am talking about. I have not explored non-Christian religious imagery to any extent but am perfectly willing to iinclude those images that anyone directs me to that bring healthy postural responses through imaging. I am sure that there are those beyond the couple I show at the beginning of the album. John

My closing thoughts (which I get to change whenever I want in this form of "book") are as follows: We have to see ourselves as whole to live a healthy life, including posturallyl. We cannot just think of ourselves as a body that has parts that go bad, knees or eyes, for instance. A body viewed as a bunch of parts is well on its way to failing. I will never forget the occasion many years ago when, after having stood in front of a mirror while working with some of my early imagery, I softly closed my eyes. Just as I did that I found myself coming up onto my tiptoes. I opened my eyes and, automatically from what I could tell, dropped back down on to my heals. Closed my eyes again and off I went again up onto my toes. It went on for quite a while until I was convinced that I had arranged myself, through release of constricting postural habits, in such a complete way that my eyelids had control over my balance and feet ! That was an occasion when I felt whole... and there was evidence that in some ways I must have been. My next closing thought is one that many of us all share... in principle. That is that our intellectual, moral, and spiritual lives, are interconnected with our bodies just as inextricably as body parts are part of a whole. This does not mean that a "beautiful" or even what we might consider a "healthy" body begets one with intellectual, moral, and spiritual health. Far from it. And the reverse is certainly not true either... that a body we do not consider beautiful or healthy necessarily suffers from intellectual, moral, or spiritual corruptness. These things are not true. But I do think that improved intellectual, moral, and spiritual health CAN and hopefully does improve structural/postural health. I also think that things that improve structural or postural health are not real unless they potentially improve a person's intellectual/moral/spritual life. My final thought at this time is that we should be thinking of the health we all seek (even though it may not appear so) requires practices and thoughts that strongly link the physical, and spiritual, etc. aspects of our lives. I think now that Christianity does do that and I also humbly think that my imagery, beyond being a "cure" for aches, pains, and disabilities, ulitmately shows us a larger world, which improves our intellectual, moral, and spiritual picture. Maybe that's enough heady thinking for a while. Anyone know a good joke?

My earliest images came about as a result of trying to conceptualize (and explain to potential students of the Alexander Technique) how the body supports itself most efficiently (against the gravitational pull). The first drawings were generally like the one at the bottom left of this group of images. I called it an ARCHETYPAL TETRAPOD (or healthy all-around four-legged land-bound creature) with the intension of showing what seemed to me the most important relationship to maintaining structure/posture on this earth. I quickly discovered that the drawing had the potential for being something more than an illustration or a metaphor. If I imagined that my dorsal surface was arched and dome-like (and light and fluffy)... and my ventral surface contracted (and dark and dense), I noticed that I rose up out of a slump and kinks in my back and elsewhere began to disappear. After a while I developed the "lamb and egg" image, perhaps my most popular one. It became clear that the relationship of the dorsal and ventral surfaces were not just to gravity, but, probably more importantly, but also to each other. That means that ALL of the dorsal surface should be able to feel and be fully expanded while All of the ventral surface is gently contracted. By expanding the dorsal more and contracting the ventral more, I could see that the ideal healthy dorsal-ventral relationship could include a baby comfortably in fetal position, a parent comfortably seated on the ground and holding a child, and even more... to be explained in the next illustrations. From the above descriptions and the illustrations you may be able to understand when I state that the ideal dorsal surface is an "innocent" one, "full of life," without tensions. And the ideal ventral surface is a "caring" one, gently "concerned" and contracted around itself or that which it cares about. AFTER drawing the first "lamb and egg" drawing, I discovered that a lamb is what that archetype looks like. And a healthy formation, both physically and spiritually, can come from imagining yourself to be the lamb and meditating on all of these images.

To most effectively experience the healthy release of crosses on our body, we should imagine that the Xs are eliminated first.
