This is a chart that covers some familiar territory... for many of you. To the left are two versions of the archetypal dorsal-ventral relationship on horizontally oriented creatures. The arrows indicate perhaps how best to absorb... and imagine... the forms. The bottom row shows three basic ways to imagine your dorsal-ventral seam. Even though they all indicate a expanded and light dorsal surface and a contracted and heavy ventral surface, imagine the changes throughout you "edges" will bring about a big variety of kinesthetic sensations and both subtle and not-so subtle postural change. What should you let yourself experience more... for your good? Perhaps, all three. Next proceed upward to experience how exaggeration of the first row's basic differences bring about even more differences. The second row takes me into theatrical extremes... and unusual stretches. Finally, the top row suggests that you experiment with both upward and downward curved curved seams out on the end of stretched seams. I experience some calm elegance and other subtle sensations and shifts from these. The example with the "L/R" and "R/L" printed is my way of suggesting that you play with ALL of your seam on the left side turning downward and all of your seam on the right side turning upward... and later, vice versa. This has been very good for me to release some of my left-right imbalance. I imagine the left seam turned downward (which brings about a sense of sturdiness) and my right seam turned upward (which gives me greater lightness and delicacy there. (My right side tends to be my assertive power side, it seems). Enough for now. Hope some of you find this valuable.

WHAT IS THE CORRECT WAY TO BREATH? This question comes up every so often and so I thought that I would come out of “retirement” and give my perspective on this matter while resurrecting an old image exercise. Basically, the PRI perspective on improving breathing is to imagine that the breath comes in and goes out through every portion of the body surface. This idea is illustrated on the smaller character at the upper left of this page’s cover picture. This general or “universal” image works as a useful image exercise, but is not sufficient to free up and form all our stubborn areas. Better breathing comes from not being habitual, not being our usual selves or “type.” If you imagine (while just breathing) that the breath comes in and goes out just as shown on each of the four larger characters, you will experience a considerable amount of postural (and breathing) change. As the old timers here know, one of the versions is your own unrecognized “method” of breathing… and it is no fun. One needs no further experience being oneself. But, also, one is your “opposite,” completely different from your style. It is, with work, a very rewarding experience. The others are good experiences as well. The experience of the three types that are not your own are beneficial and, with continued experiencing, bring about changes in muscular patterns, strengths, and releases. If you have not noticed already, the common quality for all four of the variations of “permeable and impermeable” body surface is that your breath is to only enter above in the “director segment" and to exit only from the remainder of the body. (If you do not have a strong sense of what is dorsal and what is ventral on your body’s surface, you need to learn that first here, https://goo.gl/gwzd1h , and elsewhere. In addition, this exercise is essentially further explained in the exercise, “Permeable and Impermeable”, on page 35 of https://drive.google.com/file/d/0Bz8Bej68FhX5VUg5UzFheUtqZlE/view ).

There is an area close to, but not at, the dorsal and ventral seam where it is beneficial to imagine the opposite quality (full or hollow, light or heavy, black or white, etc.) that is either univerally appropriate or type-specific appropriate.
